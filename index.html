What is a Growth Stock?

It's a share in a company expected to grow its revenue and earnings much faster than the average company in its industry or the overall market. The main goal for investors is a significant increase in the stock's price over time, known as capital appreciation.

The Engine of Growth: Reinvestment

Instead of paying out profits to shareholders, growth companies typically reinvest their earnings back into the business to fuel innovation, expand operations, and capture more market share. This strategy sacrifices short-term income for the potential of long-term exponential growth.

The Investor's Toolkit: Identifying Potential

Identifying growth stocks involves looking at both financial numbers and the quality of the business itself. A high Return on Equity (ROE) is a key indicator of management's effectiveness.

By the Numbers: Key Metrics

A high Return on Equity (ROE) shows a company is efficient at generating profit from shareholder money. Growth investors often look for an ROE of 15% or higher.

Performance Across Economic Cycles

Growth stocks are sensitive to the economic climate. They tend to soar during good times but can fall sharply during downturns due to their reliance on high expectations and investor confidence.

The chart illustrates the typical amplified performance of growth stocks. They often outperform during bull markets (economic expansion) but underperform during bear markets (economic contraction) compared to the broader market.
